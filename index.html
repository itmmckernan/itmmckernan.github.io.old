The U.S. government places encryption software on the United States Munitions List under the International Traffic in Arms Regulation (ITAR)

It therefore was designated as a weapon to be regulated for national security purposes.

1970s

Daniel J. Bernstein develops encryption software, "Snuffle", while a graduate student at University of California, Berkeley.

1990

Daniel J. Bernstein asks the State Department for permission to publish Snuffle, his encryption software.

The State Department denies his request.

1993

Bernstein and the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) sue the U.S. government for the right to publish Snuffle, claiming the regulations are unconstitutional and that his First Amendment rights permitted him to publish.

The case is heard in the Ninth Circuit District Court by Judge Patel.

1995

Judge Patel rules in Daniel J Bernsteins favor, citing the First Amendment, and declares that free speech rights protect the software's source code

April 15, 1996

Bernstein V. U.S. Department of State

It protected computer code under free speech and prohibited the barring of the export of his encryption software.

1996

President Clinton shifts oversight and licensing authority of nonmilitary encryption to the Department of Commerce

Dr. Daniel Bernstein can no longer publish his code, under the new federal relations

1996

Bernstein amends his suit to include Commerce Department.

1997

Judge Patel issues another ruling, identical to her first, reasserting First Amendment protections of encryption code.

August 1997

The U.S. government appeals those decisions

1998

A three judge panel in the ninth Circuit Court of Appeals votes 2-1 to uphold Patel's decision.

The U.S Government requests En Banc review with a full panel of 11 judges, causing the original ruling to be withdrawn.

May 1999

Before En Banc Review could occur the U.S. Government relaxes its rules on publication of Encryption Software and the case is sent back to district court.

June 1999

"Both sides filed a number of cross-motions, and in January 2002 Bernstein’s legal team renewed its constitutional challenge to the government’s encryption laws. They argued that the government’s policy violated the First Amendment and restricted research." -Kevin Featherly, Encyclopedia Britannica

2000-2002

The Federal Government backs away from certain portions of encryption rules.

October 2002

"The district court then dismissed the case on “ripeness” grounds, holding that any alleged injury to the plaintiff was hypothetical rather than actual." -Kevin Featherly, Encyclopedia Britannica

October 2003
