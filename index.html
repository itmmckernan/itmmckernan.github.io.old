Bernstein v. The United States

The U.S. government places encryption software on the United States Munitions List under the International Traffic in Arms Regulation (ITAR)

It therefore was designated as a weapon to be regulated for national security purposes.

1970s

Daniel J. Bernstein develops encryption software, "Snuffle", while a graduate student at University of California, Berkeley.

1990

Daniel J. Bernstein asks the State Department for permission to publish Snuffle, his encryption software.

The State Department denies his request.

1993

Bernstein and the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) sue the U.S. government for the right to publish Snuffle, claiming the regulations are unconstituitional and that his First Amendment permitted him to publish.

The case is heard in the Ninth Circuit District Court by Judge Patel.

1995

Judge Patel rules in Daniel J Bernsteins favor, citing the First Amendment, and declares free speech rights protect the software's source code

1995

Bernstein V. U.S. Department of State

It protected computer code under free speech and prohibited the barring of the export of his encryption software.

1996

President Clinton shifts oversight of nonmilitary encryption to the Commerce Department

Dr. Daniel Bernstein can no longer publish his code.

1996

Dr. Daniel Bernstein adds the Commerce Department to his lawsuit

1997

Judge Patel issues same ruling as before on Dr. Daniel Bernstein's Case

August 1997

May 1999

A three judge panel in the 9th Circuit Court of Appeals upholds Patel's decision. The U.S Government requests En Banc review with all 11 judges.

May 1997

Before En Banc Review happens, the U.S. government relaxes its rules on publication of Encryption Software.

The case goes back to district court with two years of arguing with cross motions.

June 1997

Dr. Daniel Bernstein's legal team renews constitutional challenge.

They claim the laws violate Dr. Daniel Bernstein's First Amendment Rights and that they restricted his research.

January 2002

Government backs away from certain portions of encryption rules.
